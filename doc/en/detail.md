## System Positioning and Feasibility

### Role of This System in Causal Inference

Athena is a **language-based hypothesis exploration system** that operates
without the quantitative datasets, identifiability conditions, or sufficient
sample sizes required by Statistical Causal Inference or
Structural Causal Models (SCMs).

More precisely, Athena aims to qualitatively approximate the boundary between
Layer 1 ("Association") and Layer 2 ("Intervention") of the Causal Hierarchy
proposed by Judea Pearl, through the integration of large language models
and web-sourced evidence.

What this system provides is not "statistical estimates of causal effects" but
**"systematic enumeration of candidate causal structures and evaluation of their relative plausibility."**

This distinction is not a design compromise but an intentional choice based on the following recognition:

> Most causal questions raised in real-world decision-making exist at a stage
> before the preconditions for rigorous identification have been established.
> At that stage, the greatest epistemological risk is not "inaccuracy of the answer"
> but "incompleteness of the question" — Athena assists in refining that question.

---

### Comparison with Conventional Methods

| Aspect | Statistical Causal Inference | Athena |
| ------ | ---------------------------- | ------ |
| Prerequisites | Quantitative data, identification assumptions | A natural language question |
| Output | Causal effect sizes and confidence intervals | Hypothesis sets with relative plausibility |
| Confounding control | Backdoor adjustment, instrumental variables | Structured collection of counter-evidence |
| Applicability | When data is available | All stages, including data absence |
| Unit of analysis | Effect sizes between variables | Explanatory causal structures |

### Achievable Value

#### 1. Comprehensive Hypothesis Space Exploration

Qualitative analysis by human experts has a structural tendency for generated
hypotheses to converge on specific explanatory frames due to confirmation bias,
availability bias, and domain-specific bias.

Athena generates multiple explanatory frames (economic, social, institutional,
cultural) in parallel within a single session and simultaneously collects
supporting evidence and counter-evidence for each hypothesis, functioning as
a **structural defense mechanism against confirmation bias**.

#### 2. Tacit Knowledge Externalization

Causal intuitions distributed across an organization typically remain as
"tacit knowledge" within individuals, making them difficult to verify, share, or update.

The knowledge graphs generated by Athena externalize this tacit knowledge as
**formal knowledge that can be verified, refuted, and updated**.
This improves the transparency of decision-making processes and shifts the
foundation of discussion from "assertions of intuition" to
"evaluation of structured hypotheses."

#### 3. Pre-analysis for Rigorous Causal Inference

Statistical causal inference using tools such as DoWhy and CausalML requires
a **prior specification of the causal graph** — namely, what to treat as
intervention variables and what to adjust for as confounders.

Athena handles the process of generating this specification, presenting
candidates for data to collect, confounders to control, and natural experiments
to design, thereby assisting in the design of subsequent rigorous analysis.

#### 4. Asymmetric Cost Reduction in Decision-Making

While qualitative causal analysis by expert teams takes weeks to months,
Athena explores an equivalent hypothesis space in minutes to tens of minutes.

This temporal asymmetry **expands the scope of causal thinking**.
It enables causal-analytical thinking — previously applied only to carefully
selected questions due to cost constraints — to be incorporated into the
context of everyday decision-making.

### Use Cases

#### Well-suited Applications

**Early-phase policy and strategy formulation**
At stages where natural experiments on policy effects do not exist or have
not yet been conducted, Athena systematizes causal explanations of intervention
candidates and identifies hypotheses that should be prioritized for verification.

**Organizational problem diagnosis**
For complex problems such as "Why are sales declining?" or "Why is turnover
increasing?" — where establishing quantitative data across an organization
is difficult — Athena generates causal hypothesis maps that integrate
explanatory frames from multiple departments.

**Research hypothesis generation**
Prior to existing literature reviews, Athena broadly enumerates the space of
causal hypotheses related to a question, providing structure for the design
of the literature review.

**Data collection design guidance**
By identifying in advance "which variables need to be measured for causal
effects to be identifiable," Athena improves the efficiency of survey design
and experimental design.

#### Poorly-suited Applications (Explicit Non-recommendations)

- Situations requiring quantitative estimation of causal effect sizes
  (e.g., calculating policy ROI, verifying pharmaceutical efficacy)
- Decision-making that requires statistical significance testing
- Use as regulatory or legal evidence

---

### Epistemological Position

The output of this system is an approximation of abduction in the Peircean
sense (inference to the best explanation) using large language models, and
it guarantees neither deductive truth nor inductive verification.

The value of the output lies not in "accuracy of conclusions" but in
**"structuring the analytical process and minimizing oversights."**

The primary design outcome metric is that using this system clarifies
"what should be verified next" rather than "what has been determined."
